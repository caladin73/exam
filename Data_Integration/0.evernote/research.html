Security Research - Phishing

Main Question:

How many percent of IBA students will try to login to a scam LMS (fake blackboard)?

Topic Description:

Modern world is surrounded by technology, almost everyone owns a device with access to the internet. While interacting with network we are sharing data which might be sensitive to us. Phishing is an attempt that tries to steal those data (often for malicious purposes) by serving us visually same page from different source. Unaware users might fall for this kind of attack. How many students are aware of this kind of thread and would not get tricked by scam page? This research is observing International Business Academy in Kolding to produce a statistics of how successful the scam might be.

Describe Key Concepts:

”How many percent” is to be able to measure the goal of the question. “IBA students” is to clarify who to target. “Will try to login to a scam LMS” is to figure out how many students are aware of online security, and to be able to recognise when not to type in personal credentials.

Resource 3: Consumer knowledge about phishing

Intel Security published an a curious study to test consumer knowledge about phishing practice and measure the ability to detect phishing emails.

According to the study, many, if not all, of your employees are unlikely to be able to spot a phishing email according to Intel, 97% of people around the world are unable to identify a sophisticated phishing email.

Furthermore the study shows that:

Only 3% got all answers right. 80% of the surveyed people got at least one wrong answer. The worldwide average score was 65.4%, which means test takers missed one in four phishing emails on average.

From this study we can backup our research study, to show that most people will not be able to spot a Phishing email.

Resource 5: Verizon data breach investigations

According to the Verizon Data Breach Investigations Report, 30% of phishing messages get opened by targeted users and 12% of those users click on the malicious attachment or link.

Within the 53,000+ incidents and 2,200-odd breaches you’ll find real takeaways on what not to do, or at the very least, what to watch for.

Verizon studies the impact of malware, DoS attacks, social engineering and other activities across multiple industries. The findings include:

  • Who’s being attacked, who’s behind them and what the primary motivators are
  • How simple errors generate a significant percentage of breaches
  • What percentage of breaches were discovered in 30 days or less

According to Verizon there is no cyber world presence without risk. But being aware of the risks, understanding them and what caused them can help you improve the security for future incidents.
